Henry backed away, pressing himself against the wall. "Christ. It’s already *in* you."

Julian touched his cheekbone. The skin was fever-hot, but beneath the heat, something pulsed—a slow, alien rhythm. "I saw things," he murmured. "In the studio. Faces peeling off canvases. Hands reaching through—"

The whiskey glass slipped from his fingers, shattering on the floor. Henry flinched at the sound. Julian barely noticed. His vision flickered—for one terrifying second, the hallway melted away, replaced by the studio’s choking darkness, the stench of turpentine and rotting pigment. Then it snapped back.

The whiskey pooled between the shards of glass, its amber surface reflecting Julian's distorted face—too pale, the eyes too dark. Henry was saying something, his voice strained, but the words dissolved into static. Julian pressed his palms against his temples, the pressure doing nothing to quiet the whispering that had started—a susurrus of voices layered beneath his own thoughts, speaking in a language that slithered just beyond comprehension.

"Julian!" Henry's shout cut through the noise. He'd grabbed Julian's shoulders, fingers digging in hard enough to bruise. "Stay with me. *Focus.*"

Julian blinked. The hallway swayed, then steadied. Henry's face was inches from his, his breath sour with last night's liquor, but his eyes were sharp with fear. "You were... glitching," he hissed. "Like a damn film reel skipping."

Julian swallowed. His throat felt lined with ash. "It's pulling me back," he muttered. He didn't need to specify what *it* was. The studio's presence clung to him, its gravity inexorable.

Henry released him abruptly, scrubbing his hands down his face. "Okay. Okay. We burn it. The whole fucking building." He spun toward the cluttered desk in the corner, yanking open drawers. "I've got property records—we can find the gas lines, the—"

Henry's fingers trembled as he unfolded a brittle city map across the desk, the paper crackling like old skin. Julian watched as he stabbed a finger at the gallery's location, his nail splitting the aged parchment. "Gas main runs right beneath it," Henry muttered. "If we can—"

Julian doubled over without warning, a wet cough tearing from his throat. Something black and viscous splattered onto the map, spreading like ink in water. Henry recoiled. "Fuck—!"

The substance moved. Thread-thin tendrils wriggled from the edges of the stain, questing toward Henry's fingertips. He snatched his hand back just as Julian collapsed onto his knees, gagging. The black mass pulsed on the paper, forming shapes—a face Julian recognized from the studio's covered canvases, its mouth stretched in a soundless scream.

Henry grabbed the fireplace poker, jamming it into the map. The paper curled and blackened, but the stain didn't burn. It *laughed*—a wet, gurgling sound that bubbled up from Julian's own ruined throat.

"Enough!" Henry hauled Julian upright, slamming him against the wall. His forearm pressed into Julian's windpipe. "Fight it, you bastard!"

Henry's forearm trembled against Julian's throat—not from exertion, but from the unnatural cold seeping through Julian's skin. Julian's lips peeled back in something between a grin and a snarl, his teeth slick with blackened saliva. "It's too late," Julian rasped—but the voice that emerged wasn't his own. It slithered, layered with whispers, like a hundred voices speaking through a single throat.

Henry recoiled, but his grip held. The hallway mirror reflected their struggle—Julian's eyes now fully black, his pupils swallowing the whites whole. Shadows pooled in the hollows of his cheeks, making his face a death mask. "Bullshit," Henry spat. He fumbled in his pocket with his free hand, withdrawing a silver cigarette lighter—a family heirloom engraved with Saint Michael slaying a dragon.

Julian's body jerked as if yanked by invisible strings. His head lolled back, tendons standing out in sharp relief as something inside him fought against Henry's hold. The lighter flared to life with a click that echoed too loudly in the confined space. "You're not taking him," Henry growled, thrusting the flame toward Julian's face.

The firelight carved grotesque shadows across Julian's features. For a heartbeat, his eyes flickered back to normal—wide, terrified, *human*. "Henry—" he gasped, before his body convulsed again. The black veins beneath his skin pulsed like living things, converging toward his wrist wound where the substance now oozed freely, forming tendrils that lashed at the air.

Henry pressed the lighter closer. The flame reflected in Julian's dilated pupils—tiny points of light in an endless void. A sizzling sound filled the hallway as the tendrils recoiled from the heat, their tips curling like burning parchment. Julian screamed—a raw, ragged sound that shook the framed landscapes on the walls.

The lighter clattered to the floor as Julian’s knees buckled. Henry caught him by the collar, barely preventing his head from cracking against the hardwood. Julian gasped, his body shuddering like a marionette with its strings cut. The black tendrils retracted into his wound with a wet slurp, leaving his skin pallid but human again.

Henry exhaled sharply, dragging Julian into the sitting room. "Stay with me," he muttered, dumping him onto the couch. Julian’s fingers twitched toward his wrist—the wound had sealed itself, leaving only a raised, ink-black scar in the shape of a paintbrush stroke.

Henry rummaged through his liquor cabinet, knocking over bottles until he found an unopened cognac from his grandfather’s collection. He cracked the seal with his teeth, spitting out the cork. "Drink," he ordered, thrusting the bottle into Julian’s shaking hands.

Julian swallowed greedily, liquor sloshing down his chin. The warmth pooled in his stomach, but did nothing to dispel the icy pressure behind his eyes. "It’s still there," he rasped. "Like... oil in water. I can feel it moving."

Henry paced before the fireplace, his shadow leaping up the wall like a caged thing. "We burn the studio. Tonight." He snatched up the city map, now stained with that unnatural blackness at the edges. "There’s a back alley access—"

Julian coughed, the cognac's burn doing nothing to chase the taste of turpentine and rust from his tongue. He flexed his wrist—the black scar pulsed faintly, as if something beneath it were listening. "They'll have security now," he muttered. "After the fire."

Henry's grin was all teeth. "Not where we're going." He flipped the map, revealing a web of sewer lines in faded ink. His fingernail—still crusted with Julian's blackened blood—tapped a junction directly beneath the gallery. "Storm drain access behind the butcher's on Rue Mortain. Leads straight to the foundation."

Julian's stomach turned. The scar on his wrist throbbed in time with his heartbeat, whispering in a language that slithered just beneath comprehension. He grabbed Henry's arm. "You don't understand. It *wants* us to go back."

The hallway mirror reflected their huddled forms—Julian's pupils swallowing the last flecks of hazel, Henry's face gaunt with sleepless terror. Outside, the streetlights flickered. Something tapped at the windowpane. Not rain. Too deliberate.

Henry didn't look. "Then we bring hell with us." He wrenched open a cabinet, scattering mothballed wool to unearth a rusted gasoline canister. The stench of stale fuel filled the room. "Granddad's lawnmower fuel. Probably napalm by now."

Henry’s fingers drummed against the gasoline canister, the hollow metal sound echoing in the silent room. Julian stared at his own reflection in the hallway mirror—his pupils were still too wide, the whites threaded with fine red cracks like shattered porcelain. The tapping at the window grew louder. Insistent.

“Don’t,” Henry muttered, tossing Julian a blackened trench coat from the hall closet. It smelled of mothballs and old sweat. “Don’t look.”

Julian looked. The window was streaked with rain—or what should have been rain. The droplets moved in rivulets too thick, too slow, forming shapes that dissolved before he could decipher them. A face pressed against the glass—just for a second—its features smeared like wet paint. Then gone.

Henry wrenched open a drawer, scattering loose screws and dead batteries. His hands closed around a pair of bolt cutters, their jaws crusted with rust. “Storm drain’s chained shut. Always is.” He tossed them to Julian, who caught them awkwardly, the metal cold against his palm.

Julian flexed his wrist. The black scar pulsed, sending a jolt of pain up his arm that made his vision blur. For a heartbeat, he wasn’t in Henry’s cluttered sitting room—he was back in the studio, staring at the unfinished portrait, watching his own face twist into something unrecognizable. Then the moment passed, and Henry was shoving a flashlight into his hands, the plastic grip slick with sweat.

The bolt cutters weighed heavier than they should have. Julian watched his fingers flex around the rusted handles—the black scar on his wrist pulsed in sync with his heartbeat, whispering in a language that made his teeth ache. Henry was stuffing rags into the gasoline canister with frantic precision, his knuckles white around the neck.

"Rue Mortain's three blocks west," Henry muttered, knotting the rags with trembling fingers. "Alley stinks like rotting meat—you'll know it." He didn't meet Julian's eyes. The hallway mirror showed Julian's reflection flickering at the edges, like a candle guttering in a draft.

Something scratched at the front door—long, deliberate strokes down the woodgrain. Henry froze mid-motion. The scratching stopped. A wet thump followed, like a body sliding down the doorframe. Julian's scar flared hot as fresh ink.

Henry exhaled through his nose. "Not real," he whispered, more to himself than Julian. The scratching resumed, slower now, each drag punctuated by a soft, wet sound—fingernails peeling back from flesh. Julian's vision doubled—Henry's sitting room overlaid with the studio's choking darkness, the scent of turpentine thick enough to taste.

Henry grabbed Julian's shoulder, his grip vise-tight. "Focus." His breath smelled of cognac and stale panic. "Storm drain. Then the foundation. Then we burn that fucking place to the bedrock." The scratching at the door intensified, the wood groaning under the pressure.

The bolt cutters slipped from Julian's grasp and clattered to the floor—an unnatural silence swallowed the sound before it reached the ground. Henry didn't flinch. His eyes were fixed on the door's brass handle, which had begun to rotate with glacial slowness. A drop of black fluid oozed from the keyhole, elongating like melted wax before splattering onto the hardwood. 

Julian's wrist burned. The scar had split open, but no blood welled up—instead, a thin curl of smoke escaped, carrying the acrid stench of linseed oil and charred canvas. Henry shoved the gasoline canister into Julian's arms and lunged for the fireplace, wrenching the iron poker free with a screech of metal. "Back door," he hissed. "Now."

They stumbled through the kitchen, Henry kicking aside empty liquor bottles that rolled like tumbleweeds. The back door's deadbolt stuck—rusted from years of disuse—and Julian's fingers slipped on the mechanism. Behind them, the front door's hinges groaned. Something wet slapped against the foyer tiles. 

Henry drove the poker into the doorjamb with a snarl. Wood splintered. Cold night air rushed in as the door burst open, carrying the scent of rain-soaked pavement and something fouler—rotting meat left in turpentine. They spilled into the alley, Julian's shoes sliding on wet cobblestones. The gasoline sloshed in its canister, its fumes mixing with the alley's stench of garbage and mildew. 

Rue Mortain was a narrow gullet of crumbling brick and boarded-up windows. The butcher's shop loomed ahead, its sign swinging on rusted chains. Henry skidded to a halt beside a storm drain, its iron grate streaked with decades of grime. The chain securing it was new—shiny steel links stark against the rust. Julian's breath came in ragged gasps as he fumbled with the bolt cutters. His wrist throbbed in time with his pulse, the black scar weeping smoke.
